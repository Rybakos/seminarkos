Mercury

Mercury, named after the Roman god of commerce, is the smallest planet in our solar system and the closest one to the Sun

It is a rocky, barren world with a diameter of approximately 4,879 kilometers, making it slightly larger than Earth's Moon.

Atmosphere Orbital Period Climate
It consists primarily of hydrogen (H₂) and traces of other elements such as sodium, potassium, and helium. The average orbital period of Mercury is approximately 88 Earth days. During the day, temperatures can rise above 400 °C, while at night, they can drop to as low as -180 °C.

Solar transits

Mercury's surface is characterized by numerous impact craters, caused by collisions with asteroids and comets. These craters vary in size and depth, and some are surrounded by rays of ejected material.One of the most prominent surface features on Mercury is the Caloris Basin, a large impact crater with a diameter of about 1,550 kilometers. It is surrounded by concentric rings and radial troughs. The surface is covered in plains, hills, and scarps. There are also features such as rupes and lobate scarps, indicating tectonic activity in the planet's past.

Mercury has an extremely thin and tenuous atmosphere, known as an exosphere. It consists mostly of atoms and molecules that have been blasted off the surface by the solar wind and impacts. The exosphere of Mercury contains trace amounts of hydrogen, helium, oxygen, sodium, calcium, and potassium, among other elements. These gases are present in very low concentrations. Due to the planet's high surface temperature and low gravity, the atmospheric particles escape into space relatively quickly, resulting in a very sparse and transient atmosphere.

Solar transits of Mercury occur when the planet passes directly between the Sun and the Earth, appearing as a small black dot moving across the Sun's disk.Solar transits of Mercury provide valuable opportunities for astronomers to study the planet's orbit and gather data about its atmosphere and surface. By observing the timing and duration of transits, scientists can refine their understanding of Mercury's position in the solar system.
